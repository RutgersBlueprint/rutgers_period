Some more facts...

  • 31 states in the United States still charge a sales tax (“pink” tax) on feminine hygiene products like pads and tampons.
  • Nadya Okamoto is the founder of PERIOD and author of Period: A Manifesto for the Menstrual Movement.
  • 2/3rds of low-income women in St. Louis could not afford pads or tampons.
  • Period poverty is defined as the lack of access to sanitary products, menstrual hygiene education, toilets, hand washing facilities, and, or, waste management (global citizen 2019).
  • Poor menstrual hygiene can cause physical health risks and has been linked to reproductive and urinary tract infections.
  • The total cost of your period over your lifetime comes to $18,171, which include the costs of what is needed to cope with pain and hassle of menstruation - like heating pads, acne medication, period products, pain relievers, etc.1 (huffpost).
  • Federal prisons now allow free access to menstrual products, state prisons do not.
  • The average menstruator uses about 17,000 pads or tampons during their entire menstruating lifetime.
  • In India, 70% of all reproductive diseases are caused by poor menstrual hygiene.
  • The Ocean Conservancy collected 27,938 used tampons and applicators on beaches around the world in a single day in 2015. So don’t flush your tampons because they don’t get filtered out!
  • According to a 2017 survey by children’s charity Plan International UK, almost half of girls in the UK missed an entire day of school because of their period.
  • Lack of access to period products is the number one leading cause of absenteeism in the US.
  • Women represent 43% of clinical trial participants globally. There is significant underrepresentation of females in research that tries to create new therapies and medical procedures.
  • A menstrual cup made of medical grade silicone can last up to 10 years!
  • Over 80% of young people across the globe cannot access the products they need on a monthly basis.
  • The average menstrual cycle lasts between 25-36 days and someone else’s normal cycle can be different from yours!
  • Menstrual cups can stay in place up to 12 hours. (Does anyone else use menstrual cups? What has your experience been like)
  • The average period releases less than a cup of blood. Most lose between a few tablespoons and a cup each month.
  • In 2011, Always was the first company to show blood in advertisements for sanitary napkins. This broke the “women bleed blue liquid” trend, but still only appeared in print.
  • Women in prison often have to beg guards for menstrual products.
  • In the United States, the average age for a girl to get her period is 12. However, this varies from person to person.
  • In one testimony from the border, a girl was detained for ten days without being offered a shower and with only one sanitary pad a day. (discuss how this makes you feel, reflect).
  • 25 million women live below the poverty line, yet menstrual products are not offered as part of food stamps.
  • Intense fluctuations in hormones can cause increased vulnerability to depression.
  • Reusing products or using tampons and pads for too long—or not using them at all—leads to higher rates of infection and other conditions for which patients are likely to require medical intervention.
  • The health risks and shame facing menstruating homeless people, who, unable to access pads and tampons, often resort to newspapers, socks, rags or even bleeding into their clothes.
  • In 2016 women represented 13% of the local jail population.
  • Endometriosis affects 1 in 10 women between the ages of 15-49.
  • A 2015 Correctional Association of New York report detailed a practice at one prison in New York which required menstruating prisoners to show their dirty pads as proof they needed extra.
  • The FDA does not require tampon and pad manufacturers to release information about the ingredients in their products, which means components like non-organic cotton, used in tampons despite high levels of pesticide use, is unregulated and unstudied.
  • Prostaglandins are the chemicals that trigger muscle contractions in the uterus and cause menstrual pain. More than half of menstruating people experience some pain around their period.
  • Many females will develop at least one uterine fibroid during their lifetime. These are noncancerous tumors developed between the layers of tissue in the uterus.
  • PMS affects as many as 3 in 4 women and includes symptoms such as headache, fatigue, bloating, and irritability.
  • In 1920, Dr. Béla Schick coined the phrase “menotoxin” for a theory he had that women produce toxins during menstruation. He reached this conclusion after a nurse who was menstruating handled a bouquet of flowers. Schick observed that those specific flowers wilted sooner than flowers the nurse didn’t touch. He decided her period was the cause.
  • By 1974, researchers had identified that menstruation taboos may be closely tied to how men participate in procreative activities. In other words, the less men are involved with childbirth and childrearing, the more distasteful a period is to them.
  • In 2018, the United Nations (UN) reported that the shame, stigma, and misinformation that surround periods can lead to serious health and human rights concerns. That’s why they declared menstrual hygiene an issue that affects public health, gender equality, and human rights.
